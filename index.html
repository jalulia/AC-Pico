Abstract

A purpose-built USB input / output carrier for indie arcade cabinets. It reads buttons, digital joysticks, Hall-effect analog sticks, quadrature spinners and pots, and drives a modest bank of cabinet LEDs — presenting to the host as a standard HID gamepad and mouse, with a serial channel for lighting. No drivers, no key-matrix, no ghosting.

PLATE 01/Overview
§ 01 / 09

What it is

one board · four jobs · one USB cable

It carries a Raspberry Pi Pico and presents to the host as an ordinary HID gamepad and mouse, with a serial channel for cabinet lighting. No drivers, no key-matrix, no ghosting — a clean translation layer between physical arcade controls and your game.

Anatomy

the whole board on one plate — hover to isolate
SPEC. AIO-PC-001 · annotatedScale — indicative
1
USB Host Link
Micro-USB to the PC. HID gamepad + mouse upstream, CDC serial downstream. USB-C planned for later.
2
Raspberry Pi Pico H · U0
The brain. A dual-core RP2040 that solves USB, power and bootloader, with PIO free for quadrature.
3
74HC165 Shift Registers · U1–U4
Parallel-in / serial-out. Four chips read 32 buttons across three GPIO lines, and daisy-chain for more.
4
J_DIGITAL
Screw terminals for every button and joystick direction. Each input is switch-to-ground with a pull-up.
5
MCP3208 ADC · U5
An 8-channel 12-bit SPI converter for sticks and pots. A second footprint (U6) doubles analog capacity.
6
J_ANALOG
3V3, ground and A0–A7 for Hall sticks and sliders. Keep signals 0–3.3 V; divide 5 V modules.
7
MOSFET Drivers + J_LED_OUT · Q1–Q10
Logic-level N-MOSFETs sink low-side. Loads sit between VLED+ and each output, dimmed by PWM.
8
J_LED_POWER
External 5 V or 12 V for lamps, behind a fuse, reverse protection and a bulk cap. Common ground with logic.
9
J_SPIN0–3
Two to four quadrature ports. A/B signals feed the Pico directly and become relative mouse motion.

Caution

Keep LED return current off the ADC ground path. Star-tie the lamp supply to logic ground at a single point, or analog readings will wander as lights switch.

Connectors dominate the cost. The silicon is cheap; it’s the screw terminals and headers that add up — so populate only the rows a given cabinet needs.
